01 — Brief

Two brands, one market,
one hard question

A global beverage brand held two distinct hydration products in Japan — one built around athletic performance, the other around natural refreshment. Both had category presence. The question was whether they were reaching the right people, and whether they were inadvertently competing for the same ones.

The brief: map the hydration consumer landscape at scale, identify segment fit for each product, and deliver targeting recommendations specific enough to act on. Not a market overview. Actual strategic direction.

02 — Methodology

Mapping
8 million consumers

Quilt.AI's ML platform ingested 8.1M+ Japanese consumers across social, search, and digital signals. Segments emerged from behaviour and psychology — not demographics — producing culturally coherent audience profiles with direct brand implications.

Step 01
Ingest
Social content, search queries, and digital behaviour data ingested across 8.1M+ Japanese consumers in the hydration category.
Step 02
Cluster
ML algorithms grouped consumers by passion points, content engagement, and hydration-adjacent behaviour — no demographic pre-filtering.
Step 03
Profile
Clusters translated into culturally coherent segment narratives — underlying need, core values, role of hydration, brand signals.
Step 04
Contextualise
Each segment assessed for alignment with both brand values and positioning — producing a priority matrix with brand-specific targeting recommendations.

04 — The segments

Seven need states,
one clear priority

Seven distinct consumer segments were identified, ranging from 301K to 2.3M consumers, each profiled across values, passion points, hydration needs, and brand affinity. The perceptual map shows where each sits — one stood clearly apart from the rest.

Key finding

The strongest unexpected alignment came from a beauty and wellness segment — consumers who think about hydration as a skincare ritual. The recommendation: layer this audience on top of existing performance targeting to expand reach without diluting the core brand.

05 — Brand strategy

Two products,
no overlap

Product A targets performance, recovery, and — counterintuitively — a beauty and wellness segment where hydration is framed as a skincare ritual, not a sports drink. Product B owns natural refreshment and mindful hydration. The audiences don't overlap, which means the two products can coexist without cannibalising each other's positioning.

Beyond the brief
Drawing on prior account context from a related project, I extended the analysis beyond the original brief to map segment alignment across two product lines rather than one — delivering differentiated targeting for each without cannibalising either brand's positioning. The original brief covered a single product. The dual-brand output was an addition that gave the recommendations significantly more strategic utility.
06 — Reflection

Honest assessment
of the method

The framework worked — but the most important thing it produced was the finding nobody anticipated. That kind of counterintuitive output is only possible when the segmentation is genuinely behavioural, not led by demographic assumptions about who uses a sports drink.

What worked
Behavioural segmentation surfaced the Glow Seekers finding — a high-alignment audience no brief would have predicted, because the method looked at what people do, not what category they'd claim. The dual-brand extension gave the output significantly more strategic utility than a single-product analysis.
What I'd do differently
The alignment scoring is values-based, not purchase-intent-based. A stronger output would validate segment alignment against sales data or brand tracker scores — giving confidence that recommended priorities are reachable, not just theoretically well-fit. I'd also push harder for quant sizing to validate segment estimates.
What I learned
A segmentation framework is only as useful as the decision it enables. The most important design choice isn't which clusters to form — it's making the output specific enough that someone can walk into a brief the following week and act on it. Frameworks that don't drive decisions are documents, not tools.

A sister brand subsequently shifted toward natural wellness positioning — consistent with the analysis. Whether my work influenced that decision or anticipated it independently, the output proved directionally correct.
