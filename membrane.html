Building the Tool
I Wished Existed

For my AI for UX Professionals module at City St George's, I designed Membrane, an AI-enhanced process layer that keeps research connected to design decisions across the full UX workflow. The problem it addresses is one I'd seen firsthand: research gets generated, handed over, and decisions get made anyway with no visible thread back to the evidence. Insights rarely travel well. They get generated, filed, and forgotten, and the work that should have been informed by them moves forward on intuition instead.

01 — The problem

Research gets done.
Then it disappears.

As AI embeds itself in UX workflows, role boundaries are collapsing — designers research, PMs design, researchers ship. The consequence is lost decision traceability: insights get disconnected from the decisions they should inform, and the first viable concept gets pursued without sufficient grounding in evidence.

Existing tools address parts of this. Dovetail structures qualitative data, Miro supports collaboration, UX Pilot accelerates output. None maintain continuity across the full workflow. The insights exist. The connections don't.

Membrane spans the full double diamond. Competitors cover partial phases — none maintain continuity across all four.
02 — The solution

A persistent layer,
not another tool

Membrane isn't a replacement for Figma, Dovetail, or Otter.ai. It sits across them — ingesting research inputs, structuring them into citable insight nodes, and making those nodes retrievable at every subsequent design decision.

The insight node is the core design idea: a structured, citable unit of research evidence that travels with the project rather than the person. Each node contains a distilled insight, supporting evidence, confidence score, and tags. Nodes form a persistent knowledge base. At delivery, they surface to support PRD generation with every output explicitly linked to its research source.

Membrane is intentionally constraint-based rather than generative. It doesn't suggest designs — it flags unaddressed nodes and prompts consideration of alternatives before converging. The goal is to slow premature closure, not automate creativity.

The user-Membrane flow. Human validation is required before nodes enter the knowledge base — AI structures, humans decide.

Under the hood, Membrane uses LLMs for insight extraction from unstructured inputs, embedding and clustering for node generation and deduplication, and RAG for retrieval at delivery — so every generated output is traceable to a specific validated node rather than synthesised from context alone.

03 — Reflection

What I took
from this

This project was inspired by Professor Radu Jianu's teaching, particularly his framing of AI as a structuring layer rather than a generator, a distinction that shaped the entire proposal. A guest talk from Micaela Dixon at Picsart added further context on how AI is actually being used inside product teams at scale.

What worked
The insight node as a design unit is the strongest part — concrete, citable, and coherent across all four phases. Grounding it in retrieval rather than generation keeps it honest: the system surfaces, humans decide. The competitive analysis held up too — no existing tool genuinely spans the full workflow.
What I'd develop further
The divergence detection logic in the Develop phase is the least resolved part. Flagging "insufficient divergence" requires defining what sufficient looks like, which is harder than it sounds. A v2 would focus here first. The target user framing also needs revisiting — I scoped Membrane for early-career builders, but the traceability problem is just as real for senior practitioners who have the skills but not the time to maintain rigour across fast-moving projects.
What I got wrong
The technical ambition outpaced the practical thinking in places. Reaching for multimodal models to compare design artefacts felt compelling on paper, but there are simpler entry points I didn't explore thoroughly enough. The proposal would have been stronger for more constraint, not more capability.
What I learned
Designing a tool that sits between other tools is harder than designing a standalone one. Every decision about what Membrane does is also a decision about what it leaves to Figma, Dovetail, or the designer. That boundary is where the most interesting design thinking happened, and where I'd spend more time if I built it for real.
