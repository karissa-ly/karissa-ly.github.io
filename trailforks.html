UX Design · Human Factors

Designing for the Rider,
Not the Route

For my Human Factors module at City St George's, I analysed Trailforks Pro (the dominant mountain biking navigation app) as a human-system interaction problem. Mountain biking is an extreme sport. It's a dangerous, safety-critical activity where errors such as missing a junction at speed could lead to real bodily harm: a bad fall, broken bones, spinal injuries, or worse. This project identified where the interface creates those risks and proposed three redesign interventions to reduce them.

01 — The problem

A pre-ride tool used
in the middle of a ride

Trailforks Pro is well-built for pre-ride planning: browsing trails, checking difficulty ratings, reviewing elevation profiles. Where it falls short is in-ride navigation. The turn-by-turn guidance is limited, and the interface was designed for a moment of calm attention that doesn't exist on a fast, technical trail.

Mountain biking demands continuous coordination between physical control and spatial awareness. Dividing attention between the trail and a screen creates the conditions for errors: misread difficulty ratings that put riders on terrain beyond their ability, missed junctions that send them down the wrong trail, and moments of interface interaction that cause instability or loss of control.

These aren't edge cases. They're predictable consequences of an interface that assumes available visual attention in an environment where it doesn't exist.

Hierarchical task analysis with risk overlay. Red nodes mark the highest-risk interaction points, where errors are most likely to occur and most consequential when they do.
02 — Interventions

Three proposals,
one principle

Each intervention restructures how existing information is presented, not what the system contains. The goal was to reduce cognitive demand at the moments when riders are least able to meet it. These are design proposals, not implemented features, sketched at intentionally low fidelity to focus evaluation on the interaction concept rather than visual execution.

Before After
Intervention 01 · Route interpretation errors
Enhanced Trail Interpretation Layer
Trailforks displays trail difficulty and condition data, but leaves interpretation to the rider. A rider who misjudges a black diamond trail or underestimates wet root sections isn't making a careless mistake. They're working with information the interface never fully translated. This intervention adds a condition-aware overlay and a "what this means" feature: not "wet conditions" but "wet conditions → reduced traction; slippery roots likely."
Design rationale: Shifts interpretation from user-driven to system-supported, reducing ambiguity and the cognitive effort required before a ride. Grounded in Norman's model of system-supported action. Comparable to Google Maps' contextual overlays, but applied to consequence rather than just visualisation.
Before After
Intervention 02 · Navigation errors at junctions
Pre-Junction Decision Support
Junction errors happen fast. Matching a digital map to a physical trail environment under time pressure, with limited visual bandwidth, is where riders go wrong. Trailforks has a junction feature (Traildar), but it requires the rider to configure and seek it out. This intervention automates the identification of high-risk decision points, delivers anticipatory cues before the junction arrives, and filters the map to show only the relevant path.
Design rationale: Shifts from user-configured to system-triggered support. Makes uncertainty visible so riders can adjust speed and attention before the decision point, not at it. Reduces reliance on recognition heuristics under time pressure.
Intervention 03 · Attention-related errors while riding
Attention-Aware Ride Mode
The root problem: the interface assumes continuous visual attention in an environment where that assumption is dangerous. Dual-task interference between navigation and bike control at speed isn't a use case to accommodate. It's a risk to design out. This intervention proposes a ride mode that activates during riding: a glanceable interface with a single directional cue, restricted interaction while moving, and suppression of everything non-essential.
Design rationale: The system adapts to reduced attentional capacity rather than competing for it. Passive guidance under high-demand conditions. Similar to Strava and device focus modes, applied here to a safety-critical navigation context where the stakes are higher. The key distinction from existing focus modes in Google Maps or Strava is context: those reduce distraction in low-stakes environments. This applies the same principle where the consequences of distraction are physical.
03 — Reflection

What I took
from this

What worked
Starting from error analysis rather than design intuition kept the interventions honest. The hierarchical task analysis forced me to map what riders are actually doing at each stage, and exposed how much the current interface assumes about available attention that doesn't exist in practice.
What I'd change
The personas were built from observation and personal riding experience rather than primary research. A stronger version of this project would involve usability testing with real riders across experience levels, especially for the ride mode, where the interaction model needs validation under actual riding conditions.
The harder question
All three interventions depend on data quality they can't guarantee. Community-generated trail information is inconsistent. An interpretation layer is only as good as what it's interpreting. The interface is fixable. The underlying data infrastructure is a harder problem.
What I learned
Designing for safety-critical environments means designing for the moments when people are least able to use what you've built. The measure of a good intervention isn't usability in ideal conditions. It's error reduction in the conditions that actually cause harm.
