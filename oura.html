My Body Was
Keeping Score

I bought an Oura ring after months of sleeping badly and not knowing why. Fifteen months later, I had 450+ nights of physiological data and a question worth asking: does this data actually tell me anything I couldn't have figured out through trial and error on my own?

01 — Context

A ring on my finger,
a year of my life

The data covers a stretch of time that included leaving a job I'd been in for four years, a working holiday in Australia living out of a van, a trip to my parents' homeland in Vietnam, some bad news, and starting a master's programme in the UK.

The visualisations that follow aren't just data plots — they're over a year of my life, quantified. I chose to work with raw physiological signals rather than Oura's composite readiness scores, which I've always been sceptical of. HRV (heart rate variability) is used throughout as the primary proxy for recovery — a raw signal, not a derived metric, and one of the most sensitive indicators of both physical and psychological stress load.

"My body was keeping score before I was. The question this project is really asking is whether making that score visible actually tells me anything I couldn't have figured out on my own."

02 — Research questions

What I was
trying to find out

Three questions shaped the analysis — each progressively more personal and harder to answer with data alone.

01
Do physiological signals shift before I consciously feel overwhelmed or stressed? If they do, how much of a window have I actually had to address and combat these feelings — and have I been using it?
02
Is daily training load a sufficient predictor of next-day recovery, or do other factors dominate?
03
Does training at specific points in my menstrual cycle affect my readiness or recovery? Can I use these findings to sketch out what a personalised activity model might look like for me specifically?
03 — Approach

How I built
the analysis

Raw data was exported directly from the Oura API and layered with self-reported tags logged throughout the period and workout data pulled from Apple Health. Rather than relying on Oura's composite scores, I worked with the underlying physiological signals to build my own picture.

Step 01
Data extraction
Pulled raw HRV, resting heart rate, body temperature deviation, and sleep data via the Oura API. Layered with Apple Health workout logs and manually coded life phase tags.
Step 02
Personalised load metric
Built a custom training load metric rather than relying on generic step count — reflecting the real distinction between movement that creates recovery demand and movement that's just part of living.
Step 03
Signal analysis
Used HRV as the primary recovery proxy. Analysed whether physiological signals shifted 3–5 days before consciously felt stress events, and whether single-day vs cumulative load better predicted recovery.
Step 04
Visualisation in Observable
Built interactive visualisations in Observable Plot and JavaScript, allowing navigation through life phases to surface patterns across the full 15-month arc.
03.5 — The visualisations

What it
looks like

Three interactive visualisations make up the core of the project. Each answers a different research question and uses a different chart form — chosen because the shape of the question demanded it.

Visualisation 01 — Life phases ridge plot
HRV distributions across eight life phases, stacked chronologically. The highlighted phase (blue) is selectable via an interactive slider. Each curve represents the full distribution of nightly HRV readings within that period — showing not just average recovery but how consistent or variable it was. The Tasmania phase is notably tighter and higher than surrounding phases.
Visualisation 02 — HRV around stress events
Each line traces HRV from three days before to two days after a logged stress event. Orange lines show cases where HRV was already falling before the event; lilac shows cases where it was rising. The bold lines are smoothed averages. A "show crying" toggle overlays additional emotional tags — a design choice that felt important to include honestly.
Visualisation 03 — HRV vs cycle average heatmap
Each cell represents one day of a menstrual cycle, coloured by how much that day's HRV deviated from the individual cycle's average. Purple = above average recovery; orange = below. Red outlines mark the first 7 days (menstruation). The handwritten aesthetic and crayon texture were deliberate — this is personal data about a personal experience, and clinical polish felt wrong.
Visualisation 04 — Period vs rest of cycle beeswarm
Each dot is a single night's HRV reading, coloured by whether I was active or resting that day. The narrower, higher distribution during the period phase versus the wider spread across the rest of cycle was one of the more surprising findings — suggesting recovery is more consistent, if lower, during menstruation.
Visualisation 05 — Training load vs next-day HRV
The scatter is the finding: there's no clear relationship between how much I trained on a given day and how recovered I felt the next morning. The quadrant labels ("Low Load, High Recovery" etc.) were added to make the null result legible rather than just confusing. A null result honestly visualised is still a result.

04 — Findings

What the data
actually showed

The findings were genuinely surprising in places — and honestly disappointing in others. That tension felt worth being honest about.

📉
There is a pre-stress physiological window — and it's narrow
HRV does dip 3–5 days before consciously felt overwhelm in several instances. The window is inconsistent enough, though, that it cannot be reliably used as an early warning system. The visualisation can show a window exists — it cannot show how wide it actually is.
🏋️
Single-day training load is a poor predictor of recovery
The scatterplot showed almost no relationship between one day's training duration and the next day's HRV. Cumulative load over 5–7 days is the more relevant variable — a limitation I'd fix with a rolling load metric in a future iteration.
🔄
Cycle phase patterns are suggestive, not conclusive
There are visible patterns in HRV and recovery scores across menstrual cycle phases, pointing toward a personalised activity model. The dataset is too small and the confounding variables too numerous to draw firm conclusions, though — this is a question worth returning to with more data.
🌏
Life phase matters more than any single variable
The most visible patterns in the data weren't training-related — they tracked life events. The Australia phase produced consistently better recovery scores than the US phases on either side of it, and the return to the UK to start the MSc brought its own distinct physiological signature. That's either the van life, the sunshine, or the absence of deadlines. Probably all three.
05 — Reflection

What I'd do
differently

The most useful part of building this wasn't the findings — it was the process of deciding what to measure, why, and what I was willing to claim. Honest data visualisation is harder than it looks.

What worked
Using raw HRV over composite scores was the right call — it kept the analysis honest and prevented me from laundering Oura's assumptions into my own. The life phase slider gave the data narrative structure it would otherwise lack.
What I'd change
Road cycling and mountain biking were both logged as "cycling" in Apple Health despite very different recovery costs. A rolling 7-day load average would be a stronger variable than single-day duration. And I'd log stress events more rigorously from day one. A planned v2 will introduce multivariate regression across cumulative load, cycle phase, and life context — and revisit whether the pre-stress window holds up under more rigorous analysis.
What I learned
Building a personalised metric is an act of interpretation, not just calculation. Every design decision in a visualisation encodes assumptions — and being transparent about those assumptions is the most important thing a researcher can do.
