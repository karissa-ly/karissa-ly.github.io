My Oura Data,
Visualised

I bought an Oura ring after a stretch of poor sleep and no obvious explanation. Fifteen months later, I had 450+ nights of physiological data and a question worth asking: does this data actually tell me anything I couldn't have figured out on my own?

01 — Context

A ring on my finger,
a year of my life

The data covers a stretch of time that included leaving a four-year job, travelling, and starting a master's programme in the UK.

I chose to work with raw physiological signals — not Oura's composite readiness scores. HRV (heart rate variability) is the primary recovery proxy throughout: a direct signal, not a derived metric.

"My body was keeping score before I was. The question this project is really asking is whether making that score visible actually tells me anything I couldn't have figured out on my own."

02 — Research questions

What I was
trying to find out

Three questions shaped the analysis — each progressively more personal and harder to answer with data alone.

01
Do physiological signals shift before I consciously feel overwhelmed — and if so, how much warning do I actually have?
02
Is daily training load a sufficient predictor of next-day recovery, or do other factors dominate?
03
Does training at specific points in my menstrual cycle affect readiness or recovery — and can the patterns sketch a personalised activity model?
03 — Approach

How I built
the analysis

Raw Oura API data, layered with Apple Health workouts and self-reported tags — working from underlying signals rather than composite scores.

Step 01 — Extract & Clean
Pulled raw HRV, resting heart rate, body temperature, and sleep data via the Oura API, layered with Apple Health workouts and manually coded life phase tags. More time than expected went into cleaning — reconciling inconsistent activity labels (road cycling and mountain biking both logged as 'cycling'), deciding which variables were worth keeping, and building a custom training load metric rather than relying on generic step count.
Step 02 — Explore & Sketch
No straight line to the visualisations. The analysis started with exploratory sketches — trying different chart forms, asking new questions as patterns emerged, and following tangents that sometimes led nowhere. Several variables that seemed promising were dropped; others surfaced unexpectedly.
Step 03 — Iterate & Focus
Exploration generated spin-off questions faster than answers. The process involved repeatedly dialling back to the original three research questions to avoid scope creep — deciding what the project was actually trying to say, and what to leave for v2.
Step 04 — Build & Finalise
Final visualisations built in Observable Plot, with design decisions made alongside analytical ones — choosing chart forms because the question demanded them, not the other way around.
04 — The visualisations

What it
looks like

Five visualisations — one per research thread.

Visualisation 01 — Life phases ridge plot
HRV distributions across eight life phases, stacked chronologically. Each curve shows the full distribution of nightly HRV — not just the average, but how consistent or variable recovery was within that period. Selectable via an interactive slider.
Visualisation 02 — HRV around stress events
Each line traces HRV from three days before to two days after a logged stress event. Orange: HRV already falling before the event. Lilac: rising. A "show crying" toggle overlays emotional tags — a design choice that felt important to include honestly.
Visualisation 03 — HRV vs cycle average heatmap
Each cell is one day of a menstrual cycle, coloured by HRV deviation from that cycle's average. Purple = above average recovery; orange = below. Red outlines mark the first 7 days. The handwritten, crayon-textured aesthetic was deliberate — clinical polish felt wrong for this data.
Visualisation 04 — Period vs rest of cycle beeswarm
Each dot is a single night's HRV reading, coloured by whether I was active or resting. Recovery during menstruation is more consistent — if lower — than across the rest of the cycle.
Visualisation 05 — Training load vs next-day HRV
The scatter is the finding: no clear relationship between how much I trained and how recovered I felt the next morning. The quadrant labels make the null result legible rather than confusing — a null result honestly visualised is still a result.

05 — Reflection

Key takeaways

The most useful part of building this wasn't the findings — it was deciding what to measure, why, and what I was willing to claim.

What I found
Single-day training load had almost no relationship with next-day HRV — the scatter was the finding. Cumulative load over 5–7 days is the more meaningful variable, which the current visualisation doesn't capture. The clearest patterns in the data weren't training-related at all — they tracked life events. The Australia phase produced consistently better recovery than the phases before and after it. That's either the van life, the sunshine, or the absence of deadlines. Probably all three.
What worked
Using raw HRV over composite scores kept the analysis honest and prevented me from laundering Oura's assumptions into my own. The life phase slider gave the data narrative structure it would otherwise lack.
What I'd change
Road cycling and mountain biking were both logged as "cycling" in Apple Health despite very different recovery costs. A rolling 7-day load average would be a stronger variable than single-day duration. I'd also log stress events more rigorously from the start. A planned v2 will introduce multivariate regression across cumulative load, cycle phase, and life context — and revisit whether the pre-stress window holds up under more rigorous analysis.
What I learned
Building a personalised metric is an act of interpretation, not just calculation. Every design decision in a visualisation encodes assumptions — and being transparent about those is the most important thing a researcher can do.
